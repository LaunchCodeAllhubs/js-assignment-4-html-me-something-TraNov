What is a TITAN motor?

  • TITAN is the US Motors line of squirrel cage induction AC motors that exceed the rating and frame guidelines set by NEMA.
  • Horizontal and Vertical Products.
  • ODP (WPI & WPII) and TEFC (TEAAC, TEWAC & XPR).
  • Roughly 200 thru 4000HP.
  • 2 thru 20 pole
  • 449 thru 9608 frame.
  • Low and Medium Voltage (up to 6900V)

Solid Shaft vs Hollow Shaft

  • Vertical Solid Shaft (VSS)
    • Constructed with solid motor shaft which is coupled to pump shaft by use of externally mounted coupling.
  • Horizontal and Vertical Products
    • Constructed with hollow motor shaft allowing pump head shaft to extend through motor shaft and connect to integrally mounted drive coupling.

Pro’s & Con’s

  • Vertical Solid Shaft
    • High HP 2-pole ratings inherently capable of up and down thrust.
    • Adjustment is more accessible due to location of coupling.
    • Eccentricity less of a problem.
    • Lower cost, but offset by higher coupling cost.
  • Vertical Solid Shaft
    • Capable of heavy down thrusts.
    • Pump head shaft extends through motor to drive coupling.
    • Less chance of vibration problems.
    • Limited 2-pole offering.

Thrust Bearing Arrangments

Angular Contact

  • Single Mount
    • Typically used for high thrust loads.
    • Capable of handling radial & axial loading.
    • Only handles thrust load in one direction.
  • Back to Back Mounting
    • Capable of handling both up and down thrust.
  • Tandem Mounting
    • Used for thrust loads up to 175%.
    • Only handles thrust in one direction.

Spherical Roller Bearings

  • Spherical Roller Bearings
    • For use on thrust loads up to 300%.
    • Uses a spring loading to prevent bearing damage during momentary upthrust.
    • Typically requires water cooling coils or synthetic oil.

Kingsbury Plate or Tilted Pad Bearing

  • Kingsbury Plate or Tilted Pad Bearing
    • For use when thrust loads are in excess of 500%.
    • Bearing life is theoretically infinite.
    • Expensive and has very high losses- needs water cooling.
    • Not good for frequent starts under thrust loads.
    • Usually avoided unless the thrust or size exceeds other capability.

Rotor Construction

  • Cast Aluminum
  • Fabricated Aluminum
  • Copper Bar

Cast Aluminum

Molten aluminum is poured into a spun cast mold, completely filling the voids, forming rotor bars, end rings and fan blades. This results in a strong, one-piece cast rotor with no joints between the bars or end rings.

No Ducts With Ducts

Fabricated Aluminum

Made with extruded aluminum rotor bars that are inserted into a stacked core. End ring molds are then attached to each end of the rotor, and the core is heated just before pouring the aluminum end ring.

No Ducts With Ducts

Copper Bar

Used for high inertia starts or specialty applications. Motors requiring high slip or torques (pulverizers, crushers, high inertia fans, etc.).

No Ducts With Ducts

Winding Methods

Random Wound is a coil made with ROUND wire. Form Wound is a coil made with SQUARE or RECTANGULAR wire. The main difference with this two winding methods is the wire type.
